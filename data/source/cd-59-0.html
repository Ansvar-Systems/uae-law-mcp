- Petroleum Geosciences; Petroleum Engineering; Chemical Engineering
Students may substitute I specialized elective with a course from the other two specializations (Petroleum Geosciences, Petroleum Eng., or Chemical Eng.) or from another approved graduate level program with the approval of the program committee.

Preface
This is the second version of the document prepared by the joint committee from the Colleges of Engineering and Sciences for a coordinated Masters program leading to M.Sc. degrees in the group of petroleum disciplines: petroleum geosciences, petroleum engineering, and chemical engineering. These disciplines are grouped together because of their relevancy and interdependency on each other, as the petroleum geosciences discipline deals with exploration methods and describing subsurface formations, the petroleum engineering discipline deals with the reservoir description, modeling, production and processing of petroleum, while the chemical engineering discipline covers crude oil processing, treatment, refining, and petrochemicals. In addition, most of the enhanced oil recovery techniques are chemical processes. Collectively, these three disciplines make up the core scientific proficiencies needed in the upstream and downstream petroleum operations. As the challenges of industry demand the integration of the efforts of these disciplines, while also keeping the respective specializations, the programs of study at the university need also to reflect this integration and specialization. This proposed program would allow its students to tackle a number of challenging research issues that require this multidisciplinary collaboration.
This proposal had been preceded by two other proposals from the Chemical and Petroleum Engineering Department and the Geology Department for their respective Masters programs. PART 1: Definition of the Program
1.1 INTRODUCTION
The disciplines of Petroleum Geosciences, Petroleum Engineering, and Chemical Engineering have special importance to the development of industry in general and the petroleum and petrochemical industries in particular. The first two are essential for the upstream (production) side of the industry, while the third one provides the technical basis for the downstream (processing, refining, and petrochemical) side of the Industry. The petroleum (crude oil and natural gas) industry provides almost 60% of the worldwide energy supplies. Technical innovations such as simulation of reservoir fluid flow, 3-D seismic, horizontal drilling, deep-sea platforms, and enhanced oil recovery (EOR) processes, have allowed us to extend the lifetime of the world's current oil reservoirs and recover a larger proportion of the oil in the ground. They also allowed us to reach deep offshore fields and made marginal fields economical. Such innovations have helped keep the reserves/production ratio almost constant for a century, and thus provided the world with a more effective, flexible, and cheap source of energy than any other source.
The processing and refining industry provides us with the different "cuts" of petroleum such as natural gas liquids (NGL), liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), gasoline, diesel fuel, naphtha, jet fuels, ...etc., that are used for different purposes. The catalytic cracking process has made it possible to extract lighter products from heavier crude oils. Chemical products like Methyl Tertiary Butyl Ether (MTBE) and Ethyl tertiary Butyl Ether (ETBE) have provided us with cleaner burning gasoline. The petrochemical industry with its hundreds of products such as nylons, polyvinyl chloride (PVC), polyethylene, and composite materials have changed the way we build our cars, planes, furniture, and even how we make our clothes. The pesticide and fertilizer industries have had a major impact on agricultural production. The gas-liquefaction industry has made it possible to transport natural gas thousands of miles by sea, therefore providing clean energy to the consumers and a source of income for the producers.
In the United Arab Emirates (UAE), the petroleum and petrochemical industries involve the production, processing, refining of crude oil and natural gas and the manufacturing of chemical and petrochemical products' Revenues from the petroleum industry constitute about two-thirds of the nation's budget, while the chemical and petrochemical industries provide about 70% of the value of industrial production. Between them, they provide over a third of the nation's gross national product (GNP). These industries form and will continue to form the backbone of economic activity in the country now and in the future, due to their competitive advantage in the UAE relevant to a worldwide market This unique position for the industry in the country makes strong ties between the relevant departments of the University and the industrial sector (both governmental. and private) an issue, that should be given high priority.
The UAE University has recently placed a large emphasis on research and graduate studies relevant to the different needs of the country. New interdisciplinary graduate-level programs have been initiated .such as Environmental Science, Materials Science and Engineering, Water Resources, and the Master of Business Administration (MBA). Also, proposals for new programs have been invited from different colleges and departments in the fields of education. Science, Engineering, Humanities, Business, Law, ... etc, and a few of these new programs are expected to start within the next three years. Funding of research, both internal and external, has also become more significant Moreover, the number of bachelor-degree graduates is increasing steadily each year.
The two colleges of Sciences and Engineering at UAE University have gone through major reorganizations and development since their inception about 25 years ago. The College of Engineering has established new buildings comprised of offices and departmental lab facilities in 1990. The buildings contain over 40 labs, for both general engineering and departments, 8 of which are for the Chemical and Petroleum Engineering Department, The Engineering College development culminated in its undergraduate programs in Chemical and Petroleum along with the Civil, Electrical, Electrical (Communications), and Mechanical obtaining recognition as "Substantially Equivalent" International programs to their U.S. counterparts from the Accreditation Board for Engineering and Technology (ABET) in 1998/99. The college of Sciences has also undergone a major laboratory modernization effort with new facilities established. The two colleges already collaborate in running the University's applied science M.Sc. programs in Environmental Sciences, Material Science & Engineering, and Water Resources.
Within this framework, and with the anticipated need in the country to retrain and/or advance the qualifications of specialists in the fields of petroleum geosciences, petroleum engineering, and chemical engineering, it is proposed to start this Masters program in a coordinated manner. The reason for offering the program with the three sub-disciplines together is that they give each other more flexibility to answer the needs of an increasingly integrated industry. The petroleum geoscientists and petroleum engineers work hand-in-hand in characterizing petroleum fields and devising "optimum" methods for recovery of petroleum reservoirs. While the petroleum engineers and chemical engineers work closely on production and processing units such as facilities to separate oil, gas, and formation waters, and transporting these to further refining, or gas liquefaction plants. The three disciplines also work together on relevant environmental issues. Furthermore, a number of petroleum engineering processes are based on chemical engineering principles, such as the majority of EOR processes. Offering these disciplines together in one program provides an opportunity to offer the industry, through the M.Sc. theses, integrated research solutions. These solutions may only be delivered through coordinated work of specialized students supervised by faculty from more than one of the three different but related disciplines. Each degree program will retain its distinctive specialized nature that the industry recognizes through specialized core and elective courses, while a small group of common core and general courses along with a coordinated graduate seminar will introduce students to the issues facing the industry and how their specialties may contribute to the solution of these issues. The three-coordinated sub-disciplines are designed primarily for graduates of geology, chemical engineering, and petroleum engineering; However, graduates of other branches of sciences or engineering disciplines,, especially the ones that require retraining for different career paths in the industry, will also be admitted.
The M. Sc. degrees to be offered are designed to share one sequence of common core courses for 10 credit hours including a Graduate Seminar for 1 credit. Thereafter, the students take discipline-related core (9 credits) and elective (6-15 credits) courses. The topic for the 6-credit hour thesis could be specific to the disciple or interdisciplinary with co-advisors from the other two disciplines available. This is one of the important benefits of coordinating the offering of this program. Students obtain their M. Sc. degree upon completion of the required 31-34 credits. Degree requirements including course descriptions are fully described in Part 2 and the program template is listed in Table 2,5. Out of the 31 courses described in the proposed program, 6 are interdisciplinary courses with the code (PTSE) symbolizing the program name, 8 are Geology (GEOL), 8 are Chemical Engineering (CHME), 7 are Petroleum (PETE), and the remaining 2 are Mathematics (MATH) and Statistics (STAT) courses.
All degrees require the submission of a written thesis on a research topic and successfully passing an oral defense of the thesis. The language of instruction is English and, therefore, proficiency in English language is an admission requirement.
1.2 GOALS AND OBJECTIVES OF THE PROGRAM:
The objectives of this graduate program are as follows:
1) Graduate scientists and engineers with a high level of competence in fundamental and applied concepts of petroleum geosciences, petroleum engineering, or chemical engineering.
2) Enhance the students professional and research experience in the broad fields of petroleum geosciences, petroleum engineering, or as applied research topics are selected with relevance to the industry's interest.
3) Enrich the collaboration in research and development between the University and the national and industrial sectors in the country and worldwide.
The goal of this program is to graduate master-level petroleum geoscientists, chemical and petroleum engineers who are highly qualified for a rewarding professional experience. Graduates may choose to work in the petroleum industry, chemical, or petrochemical industries, continue on to other graduate level degrees, or join consulting/contracting companies. PART 2 : The Academic Program
2.1 DEGREE TITLES
Candidates, depending on their study program, may obtain one of three degrees:
Master of Science in Petroleum Geosciences
Master of Science in Petroleum Engineering
Master of Science in Chemical Engineering
2.2 LANGUAGE OF INSTRUCTION
English is the language of instruction for the program
2.3 ADMISSION REQUIREMENTS
For admission to the respective M. Sc. program, a B. Sc. degree in either Geology, Chemical Engineering, or Petroleum Engineering is required with a minimum cumulative Grade Point Average (GPA) of 2.5 out of 4 and Very Good (3.0 out of 4.0) in technical courses relevant to specialization. Applicants with a B. Sc. degree in other engineering or science branches and satisfying the GPA requirements are also eligible for admission. This admission is, however, provisional pending the completion of deficiency courses prior to or during the study program as set by the executive committee of the program. Also, candidates from Geology, Petroleum Engineering, or Chemical Engineering who wish to study one of the other two disciplines are also required to complete deficiency courses. Passing deficiency courses is necessary as knowledge of geology; chemical, and petroleum-engineering fundamentals in addition to competence in Math and Computer applications are prerequisites for taking most graduate courses in the three disciplines. The list of deficiency courses will vary depending on tile applicant's background and experience.
Other requirements for admission include:
* Proficiency in the English language as demonstrated by passing the TOEFL exam with a score of 500 or "better. This requirement may be waived for graduates from English-speaking countries. A score between 400 and 500 in TOEFL will necessitate taking remedial one to two English courses.
* Two letters of recommendation.
2.4 ACADEMIC ADVISING GUIDELINES
Advisement of all new graduate students in the program is to be carried out by the Executive Committee of the program composed of representatives from the Geology Department and the Chemical & Petroleum Engineering Department. During his/her first semester, the student should register in the Graduate Seminar and is expected to choose the subject of research for his/her thesis or independent study, and consequently the faculty acting as principal advisor. Students are advised to discuss graduate supervision with all faculty members working in the chosen research area before making their decision. The principal advisor, then, becomes the student's academic advisor for all academic other academic functions such as registration.

In order to obtain the M. Sc. degree in Petroleum Geosciences, Petroleum Engineering, or Chemical Engineering, the candidate must fulfill the following requirements:

• Taking the Common Core Courses shown in Table 2.1 for 10 credit hours.
• Taking the relevant Specialized Core Courses as shown in Table 2.2 for 9 credit hours.

PTSE 700
Graduate Seminar (I credit)
This is a pass-or-fail core requirement that. involves presentation and discussion of current research topics in Petroleum Science and Engineering Faculty members and expert visitors and industry professionals about local and worldwide significant research issues, with student presentations of progress in their research topics, contribute the seminars. Crude Oil Characterization (3 credits)
Gross oil composition. Molecular properties of crude oil. Source input and depositional environment of crude oil. Maturity of crude oil. Effects of migration on the biological markers of oils. Initial and boundary value problems for ordinary differential equations. Formulation and numerical solution of parabolic, elliptic, and hyperbolic PDE's using finite difference discretizations. Applications in petroleum sciences and engineering.

Source Rock Characterization (3 credits)
Origin of fossil fuels, Chemical structures and conventions. Formation , composition and maturation of Kerogen. Composition of bitumen, petroleum and natural gas. Analysis of geologic controls on composition and architecture of oil and gas reservoirs, with emphasis on reservoir heterogeneity resulting from depositional and diagenetic processes. Reserve estimates for gas and gas-condensate reservoirs; gas well performance; gas-well testing, gas flow in transmission lines; gas storage fields; liquefied natural gas.

Petroleum Geo-sciences specialization
PTSE 721
Adv. Reservoir characterization
Interpretation of data that delineate reservoir dynamics and conceptualize reservoir architecture. Data collected from drilling-logs, detailed petrographic core studies, and open- hole logs. Additional data such as tracer study, characterization of water chemistry, and data from MWD and their use to understand lateral and vertical variation in reservoir properties, identify cross zone communication, vertical permeability profile, and permeability anisotropy. Integrated use of data to characterize a reservoir, produce a simulation model, and predict its potential performance.
GEOL 721
Basin Modeling (3 credits)
The modeling of sedimentary basin takes many different forms including geological modeling, structural modeling, Geochemical modeling, and reservoir simulation. In this course students will be exposed to these different types of basin models, the required input data for each of these model types, and the methodology for modeling as well as the application of the result of each model. The student will learn how to incorporate data from basin analysis, reservoir characterization, petrophysical data, structure data, and reservoir geochemistry into a reservoir simulation.
GEOL 722
Advanced Structural Geology (3 credits)
The aim of the course is to extend and apply structural principles to interpret fold and fault systems and their influence of fluid migration (gas, petroleum, water) in the crust. The topics covered include: the development of brittle structures and their interpretation; fluid migration and fluid-rock interaction in the upper crust; geometry of large scale fault systems; fold mechanisms; tectonically characteristic associations of folds and faults; salt tectonics; the structure of the Arabian "Peninsula with special reference to the UAE petroleum fields; stereographic methods in drill-core structural analysis.
(Pre-requisite: GEOL 2852 Structural Geology or equivalent)

4.1 Comparison for the M. Sc. Petroleum Geosciences Degree;
The UAEU program of courses for petroleum geosciences compares well with parallel programs in the Universities of Texas, Oklahoma, New Orleans, Pernn State and Stephen Austin University (Table 4.1). The courses most popularly offered at all of these universities, and also represented at UAEU, are petroleum geochemistry, basin modeling and advanced structural geology. Almost equally represented are courses on crude oil characterization, statistical methods, reservoir geology and characterization, and environmental impact assessment for petroleum industries.
The main differences or highlights of the UAEU program are the inclusion of topics such as Advanced Petrophysics, and applied mathematics; and to a lesser degree seismic exploration and stratigraphy. The petrophysics topic is an important issue in both the determining the supply and exercising good management of petroleum field, especially in the mature fields. To put my comments in proper perspective, I point out the following:
I have performed a similar evaluation for the Universidad Autonoma de Nuevo Leon in Mexico. In that case I was hired to restructure completely their Chemistry and Chemical Engineering graduate programs.
I have been in charge of ABET accreditation for our department for the past two cycles.
I served five years as Associate Dean for Graduate Studies, College of Engineering, The University of Texas at Austin.
I have divided my comments into areas of (1) Faculty, (2) Facilities, (3) Student Issues, and (4) Program Structure. In each case, where appropriate, I commented on the strengths and weaknesses.
FACULTY
STRENGTHS
1. There appears to already have established research activities and there appears to be research collaborations between faculty from the three disciplines. The proposed plan whereby each research project will be co-supervised by two faculty members from two disciplines is a good idea, especially since collaborative research already exists.
2. The research currently conducted by the ChE faculty covers most of the important areas (see point 1 immediately below).
3. As suggested on page 35 of the proposal and point l immediately below, the number of faculty needs to be expanded. This expansion needs to carefully planned in order to address future research /teaching needs.
WEAKNESSES
1. Two ChE research topics of rapidly growing interest internationally are nano-technology and molecular engineering. Both of these areas promise to have significant impact on the petro- chemical industry. The program needs to be expanded in the future to include faculty with expertise in these areas.
FACILITIES
STRENGTHS
1. The facilities for the ChE component of the program (computational and laboratory) appear to be excellent.
STUDENT ISSUES
STRENGTHS
2. The size of the program is proposed to be 40-50 students. With over 30 faculty involved in the program and the fact that each student is to be co-supervised by faculty from two disciplines, each student will receive more than adequate personal supervision. Indeed, the graduate student to faculty ratio is rather low by international standards, unless there is also a PhD program that occupies a portion of the facilities and the faculty time. If there is a PhD program, then the 40-50 students is appropriate. If there is no PhD program, then perhaps the MS program can grow in the future.
3. The requirement of English proficiency is wise.
4. Requiring a GPA of 3.0 in graduate courses to graduate is wise and in keeping with international programs. However, assuming that graduate courses are taught at a more rigorous and demanding level than undergraduate courses, it is not advisable to admit students with a 2.0 undergraduate GPA. (see weakness l below)
WEAKNESSES
1. I strongly disagree with having the admissions requirement a minimum GPA of 2.0. If a quality program is to be established, the admissions requirement needs to be raised to a minimum of 3.0. If you establish a high standard for admissions, you can demand a high standard throughout the program and a high standard for graduation. With a minimum GPA of 2.0 for admission to the program, there will be a broad spectrum of students in any class — 2.0 to 4.0, which is too broad to allow the faculty to teach at a high graduate level.
PROGRAM STURCTURE
STRENGTHS
1. Having the program include core courses from all three areas plus core and elective courses from the specialized area is wise. This structure, along with the two credits of general electives, will provide the students with the broad-based exposure needed to function properly in teams when they enter the professional work force.
2. In preparing the proposal, the organizers have surveyed a spectrum of organizations that represent the parties who stand to benefit from the new program. Obtaining "this input was wise. I recommend that the program, once it is established, maintain communications channels with these institutions. The best way to do this is to establish an advisory committee. Most engineering departments in "North America (and elsewhere) have such a committee to help them in a variety of ways. (If you wish, I can provide more details in the future and help establish such a committee).
WEAKNESSES
1. While the core and elective courses reflect some of the research activities of the ChE faculty, there is one obvious omission in the course listing: biotechnology/biochemical engineering. This an important research area of growing importance that will probably have a great impact during the professional careers of your current and future students. Courses should be made available so that students will at least be conversant in these topics and will know the advantages and disadvantages of bioprocessing.
2. Comment 1 immediately above also applies to environmental engineering. The introduction to the proposal document mentions that an environmental engineering effort exists on campus. The proposed interdisciplinary program will be strengthened by making sure all of its graduates are exposed to at least one course in environmental engineering.
3. Chemical engineers, more than any other engineers, need expertise in separation processes. Perhaps separation processes are included in the phase equilibria course (PTSE 706) or mass transfer (CHME 711). If not, an elective or core course should be-created.

I have been in charge of ABET accreditation for our department for the past two cycles, and based on that experience I can say that nothing in the proposal documentation addresses assessment processes. {If you wish, I can provide more details in the future and help you with ABET accreditation.}
In summary, I believe the proposed interdisciplinary graduate program is an excellent idea. The program will be unique and provide a valuable service to all parties concerned (students, faculty, the university, industry, and the "United Arab Emirates.) I recommend establishing such a program, and I hope that my suggestions above will help strengthen an already promising program.
