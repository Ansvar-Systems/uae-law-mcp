Regulatory Policy
Promoting the Universal Provision of telecommunications services in the UAE
Version 1.0
Issue Date: 12 December 2017
Telecommunications Regulatory Authority (TRA)
P O Box 26662, Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates (UAE)
www.tra.gov.ae
1- Purpose and Scope of this Regulatory Policy
1.1 Article (12) of Federal Law by Decree No. (3) of 2003, as amended, Regarding the Organization of the Telecommunications Sector (the “ Telecommunications Law”) establishes the Telecommunications Regulatory Authority (the “TRA”) as the competent body to oversee the telecommunications sector in the UAE.
1.2 Article (13) of the Telecommunications Law mandates the the TRA shall exercise its functions and powers to, inter alia, (i) ensure that the Telecommunications Services provided throughout the UAE, are sufficient to satisfy the public demands of those who wish to make use of such services; (ii) enhance the level of service provided by the telecommunications sector in order to promote the interests of subscribers; (iii) encourage, promote, and develop the telecommunications and information technology industries in the State; and (iv) promote and enhance the telecommunications system in the State as indicated by the availability of the latest apparatus, equipment, and facilities provided by telecommunications technology.
1.3 Article (14)(3) of the Telecommunications Law specifically grants to the TRA the power to issue regulations, instructions, decisions and rules regulating Universal Service.
1.4 Currently, the TRA’s policy for promoting the universal provision of Telecommunications Services is captured in a number of different regulatory instruments. However, to date, there has been no Universal Service vision or Policy statement for the telecommunications sector of the UAE. This Regulatory Policy therefore sets out the TRA’s vision, rationale and policy framework for introducing and implementing a Universal Service Policy for the sector, taking into account the existing legal and regulatory framework as well as applicable UAE Government Policies.
1.5 In due time the TRA, where necessary, may issue other appropriate regulatory instruments so as to implement or facilitate the implementation of the specific policy measures set out herein. This Regulatory Policy does not replace Universal Service provisions currently set out in other legal or regulatory TRA instruments, such as, without limitation, the Consumer Protection Regulations of 2015 or the Regulatory Policy on Directory Information of 2009.
The Structure of this document
1.6 The remainder of this Regulatory Policy is structured as follows:
- Section 2 defines terms used in this Regulatory Policy.
- Section 3 sets out the TRA’s vision and rationale for introducing a Universal Services Policy.
- Section 4 defines the scope of the Universal Service Obligation (USO) both in terms of the services included and in terms of the areas that shall be covered.
- Section 5 sets out the TRA’s position on how the USO policy will be implemented, including a mechanism for choosing a USO Provider.
- Section 6 addresses the issues of USO funding.
- Section 7 addresses the issues of affordability and accessibility of the Telecommunications Services in the UAE.
2- Definitions
2.1 All words, terms and phrases used in this Regulatory Policy shall have the meanings ascribed to them, if any, in the Telecommunications Law, unless otherwise provided for in this Policy. The following terms and phrases shall have the meanings ascribed to them below when used in this Policy:

Word, term or phrase
Definition
Consumer
Any person, whether a natural person or a legal entity but excluding a Licensee, which person consumes or may consume a Public Telecommunications Service provided by a Licensee.
Universal Service (US)
The provision of a defined minimum set of Telecommunications Services to all Consumers at an affordable price.
Universal Service Implementation Plan
The Plan to be developed by the Universal Service Provider in accordance with this Policy.
Universal Service Obligation (USO)
An Obligation imposed on a Licensee to provide Universal Services.
Universal Service Provider (USP)
A Lincensee determined by the TRA to implement a Universal Service Obligation
Universal Service Policy

Public Organisations

3- The TRA’s Vision, Policy Direction and Objectives
3.1 In recent years, Licensees in the telecommunications sector have already made significant progress in providing Telecommunications Services (both basic and advanced) to Consumers in the UAE.
3.2 Licensees have deployed advanced network infrastructures and as a result, the UAE is a global leader in terms of fibre broadband availability and take up, having 75% of households connected to fibre broadband networks and over 90% of homes passed by fibre.
3.3 The fibre broadband networks have been rolled out by two Licensees in the UAE - Etisalat and du. Etisalat’s fibre network covers the majority of populated areas in the country, while du has rolled out its fibre network in newly developed areas of Dubai. Since October 2015, both Licensees have been able to offer services to customers through reciprocal bitstream access, giving Consumers a choice of Licensee for their fixed line voice and broadband services.
3.4 Both Etisalat and du use these networks to offer triple-play packages, bundling fixed telephony, broadband and TV services. Take up of multi-play packages is significant – at the end of 2015, 23% and 61% of Etisalat and du’s fixed broadband subscribers in the UAE had double-play and triple-play bundles, respectively.
3.5 Despite these positive trends, the TRA is concerned that a number of Consumers within the UAE do not have access to the same level and quality of Telecommunications Services as Consumers in the main population centres. This is largely driven by the fact that rolling out infrastructure in some rural harder-to-reach areas is more challenging, both financially and practically than elsewhere in the country. As a result, this has meant that infrastructure has not been deployed universally across the UAE to the same standard, thereby depriving Consumers in such areas of the same level of services that those living in urban areas can enjoy.
3.6 The TRA recognises that this is not in accordance with its mandates under the Telecommunications Law and Government policies that require that no citizen is excluded from the opportunities afforded by high quality access to Telecommunications Services.
3.7 Indeed, access to Telecommunications Services, including today high-speed broadband, is no longer regarded as a luxury and has arguably become a necessity. This means that Consumers should not be denied access to Telecommunications Services based on their geographic location, particularly given that more and more information and services are delivered online, such as Internet banking, e-health, e-commerce, online learning, e-government. To do otherwise would not promote fairness and social inclusion.
3.8 In addition, there is a considerable body of evidence demonstrating that having access to high-speed broadband productivity, improves labour participation (through remote working), promotes SME creation, and ultimately contributes to economic development and growth. This is likely to be particularly important for rural areas, where opportunities for economic development and growth might otherwise be more limited than in the main population centres of the UAE.
3.9 In light of the above and the Federal Government’s Vision 2021 for the UAE, the TRA has developed this Policy so as to ensure that, in line with Article 13 of the Telecommunications Law, the Telecommunications Services provided throughout the UAE are sufficient to satisfy the needs of those who wish to make use of such services; that these Telecommunications Services are enhanced in order to promote the interests of subscribers; encourage, promote, and develop the telecommunications and information technology industries in the State, and promote and enhance the telecommunications system in the State through ensuring the availability of the latest facilities provided by telecommunications technology.
3.10 In addition and with this Policy, the TRA also seeks to meet the needs of the Consumers and the UAE more generally. The Vision of the Federal Government has set the key themes for socio-economic development for the country and has at its heart the objective of making, by 2021, the UAE one of the best countries in the world in which to live. This translates into concrete objectives for the main sectors of the economy, including telecommunications, with the specific objectives that in this sector the UAE is:
- to be ranked among the top 10 countries by 2021, based on a Networked Readiness
Index (measured by the World Economic Forum); an index which captures (among other factors) the availability of infrastructure, usage of ICT and the economic and social impact of the telecommunications and ICT sector
- to be ranked fisrt on the Online Service Index, which focuses on the evolution of e-government services (smart services) in terms of availability, quality, connectivity and diversity of channels and the use by the public of these services.
3.11 Wide availability of Telecommunications Services (in particular, ultrafast fixed broadband and high-speed mobile broadband) is clearly key to achieving these objectives.
3.12 The TRA therefore is introducing measures to ensure the universal availability of, and accessibility to, a minimum set of Telecommunications Services sufficient to support the above mentioned objectives. These shall address specifically access to high speed data and other Telecommunications Services more generally in sparsely-populated areas. This document therefore sets out the TRA’s Universal Services (US) policy, on which any further necessary regulatory measures shall be based.
4- Definition of Universal Service
4.1 In this section, the scope of the proposed Universal Service Policy is defined in terms of the services that should be included and the areas where these services should be provided.
4.2 As a general principle, the TRA recognises the importance of ensuring that Consumers throughout the UAE have access to the same minimum set of services, at the same price, regardless of their location. In practice, this means that all Consumers in Permanent Dwellings should have access to services which are capable of delivering basic voice, TV services, and high speed data packages of at least 10Mbps. Furthermore, this suggests that as services are upgraded in urban areas, these upgraded services should also be made available throughout the UAE. As such, the TRA would expect the minimum broadband speed required under this Policy to increase in line with increases in the average speed for the majority of consumers in the UAE.
4.3 This principle will mean that:
- A Licensee who has an obligation to provide Universal Services must satisfy any request by Consumers residing in a Permanent Dwelling to deliver the Universal Services, as defined by the TRA, which shall include those described above at a uniform standard price; and
- A Licensee who has an obligation to provide Universal Services in a particular area must continue to provide the Universal Services described above to all Consumers in that area, together with all Public Organisations in that area, at uniform standard prices, unless that Consumer voluntarily seeks to disconnect its service or the service is legally terminated say for failure to pay outstanding invoices.
4.4 However, extending high-speed broadband coverage to so-called ‘uneconomic areas’ would also impose costs on the telecommunications industry (this is discussed in detail in Section 5 below). Therefore, it is important to ensure that any Universal Service Obligation is delivered in an efficient way which maximises net benefits to society (i.e. total additional benefits net of additional costs).
4.5 The benefits to society of this Universal Service Policy are likely to be maximised if the advanced broadband services are delivered by a technology capable of ultrafast speeds and characterised by high resilience and high Quality of Service (QoS). While there are several potential technologies which could satisfy these criteria, fibre (FTTH) stands out due to its future proof nature (as it is capable of delivering speeds of 1Gb/s and above). Therefore, the TRA’s preference is that fibre (FTTH) networks should, where feasible, be deployed to all Consumers and Public Organisations in the UAE. This has the advantage that once a Licensee deploys its fibre network, there is no reason why that Licensee could not offer higher data speeds to its customers, meaning that as Telecommunications Services are enhanced in one part of the UAE, the incremental cost of offering the same Telecommunications Services in the rest of the UAE is likely to be limited. In areas where there are physical constraints on fibre deployment (for example, where surfaces are not levelled), a combination of other technologies – fixed wireless access and satellite – may be used as long as they satisfy the USO definition (set out above), continue to do so in the future (as the USO speed requirement increases over time) and are approved by the TRA as part of the Universal Service Implementation Plan which shall be developed in accordance with this Policy.
5- Mechanisms for implementing the Universal Service Policy; the process for identifying the Universal Service Provider(s)
5.1 In order to ensure this Universal Service Policy is implemented and its objectives are met, the TRA will issue a subsequent instrument to designate one or more Licensee as having an obligation to provide Universal Services in the UAE. This provider or providers shall then be known as the Universal Service Provider (USP) and shall be obligated to meet the needs arising from the Universal Service Obligation (USO) placed upon it.
5.2 Given the scope of the Universal Service Policy described in the previous section, in designating a USP the TRA will take into account, inter alia, the existing networks of the Licensees and their geographical coverage, whilst it will also consider whether one Licensee is in a position to extend its existing networks significantly more quickly, and at lower cost, than other providers.
5.3 In some cases the USP, if appropriate, may be allowed to subcontract to other Licensees the provision of some of the Telecommunications Services which it is obliged to supply under its USO. Such circumstances may arise if – by way of example – broadband services in some areas need to be provided through satellite, in which case the USP could enter into an agreement with a Licensee holding an appropriate licence to offer, on behalf of the USP, the relevant services. Any such arrangements will, however, need to be approved by the TRA before being implemented.
5.4 For the avoidance of doubt, the USP will not by its USP status be entitled to exclusivity in any particular area that it may be obliged to cover. Thus, if another Licensee is planning to roll out telecommunication services in an area currently not being covered (e.g. in a new settlement), it will not be precluded from doing so by virtue of the fact that a USP is already covering it Furthermore, any obligations imposed by the TRA on Licensees to provide network access services nationally, such as bitstream access, shall also apply in areas where network coverage has been expanded as a result of the USO placed on the USP.
5.5 Following the USP’s designation, it shall be required to work with the TRA, local Government municipalities and such other entities as may be specified by the TRA to develop a Universal Service Implementation Plan to satisfy the parameters of this Policy. This Plan shall include the services, geographical areas and the technologies which shall be used by to provide Universal Services and the planned delivery times. It shall also include all such details and procedures necessary to ensure its effective implementation. The Plan must be completed and submitted to the TRA for approval within [12 months] of the Licensee’s designation as the USP.
6- Universal Service funding
6.1 The TRA recognises that the USP is likely to face a net cost in meeting the USO, as a result of the costs it will incur in deploying and maintaining network infrastructure in areas where otherwise it might have not rolled out its network. Depending on the scale of this net cost it may be appropriate to compensate the USP for the costs incurred in relation to its fulfilment of the USO. The source of the funds from which such costs may be covered is referred to as USO funding.
Funding mechanism
6.2 There are three potential approaches to USO funding:
- Self-funding by the USO provider;
- Industry funding; and
- Government subsidy
6.3 The TRA recognises that each of these methods could be appropriate under certain circumstances. For example:
- Self-funding by the USO provider – this can be appropriate if the cost of the USO is immaterial or the USP does not face effective competition in the market. This is because in the latter case, the USP can cross-subsidise the USO costs through the returns it makes on serving other Customers or providing other Telecommunications Services. However, if the USP faces effective competition from other Licensees, cross-subsidisation might be prohibited or may not be possible as profits in low cost areas will be competed away.
- Industry funding – the net costs of the USO may also be met through an industry fund, with contributions coming from some or all market participants, for example in proportion to their revenue market shares.
This mechanism has been used extensively to fund fixed telephony Universal Service policies in many countries around the world. This means that the net costs of meeting the USO are shared by the industry (rather than levied on one provider). However, industry funds may be complex to set up and administer and may even create market distortions compared to government funding, as typically such contribution requirements do not apply to the over-the-top and other non-traditional telecommunications players who may access the networks.
- Government subsidy (grant) – the USO net costs may also be covered by government through a subsidy. This may be appropriate if the USO net cost is significant, and therefore could otherwise place an unfair burden on the USP. In this context, it is critical to ensure that any public subsidy does not displace private investment and is not used to place the USP at an advantageous position over other Licensees. The costs of the USP (to be compensated either by government or by industry funding) need to be scrutinised by the regulator to avoid over-recovery.
6.4 The TRA shall at an appropriate time following the completion of the Universal Service Implementation Plan, publish an appropriate instrument setting out, inter alia, in detail how the USO shall be funded, when and how the USP may apply for the relevant funds etc., building on the principales described in this section of the Policy. However, unless it can be shown that the USO creates an unfair burden on the USP then the TRA would expect that the USO should be funded by the USP, such as from the returns it earns in offering other services in the UAE.
6.5 If the net cost of meeting its USO is material and risks placing an unfair burden on the USP, the USP will be able to apply for additional funding. In its application, it will have to provide calculations of the USO net costs (based on the methodology discussed below and which will be set out in more detail in the regulatory instruments to be issued pursuant to this Regulatory Policy). The TRA would review the USP’s application and, if approved, external funding will be granted.
Estimating the net cost of the USO
6.6 The USO will require the USP to maintain existing fibre connections across the UAE, as well as extend its network in line with the Policy set out herein and within the Implementation Plan. However, the TRA considers that it would be reasonable for any USO funding to only relate to the net costs incurred by the USP in extending its networks beyond the point at which it would anyway decide to deploy the required infrastructure. That is, USO funding will not be available to support the USP’s ongoing costs of maintaining its current network coverage or to cover planned expansions.
6.7 More specifically, any funding shall only be used to fund rollout of the required services in areas which are currently unserved (no high speed broadband connectivity) or underserved (where there is limited high speed broadband connectivity) and where Licensees are not definitely planning to roll out fibre networks in the near future and would incur a net cost to do so.
6.8 In examining the net cost of the USO and any application from the USP for funding, the TRA shall take into account, inter alia:
- capital investment required to serve the area, including a fair return on capital, and which may include fibre cables, optical distribution frames (ODFs), optical network termination (ONT) and, (in some cases, trenching); as well as investment in masts (if additional masts need to be installed), RAN equipment and backhaul – for fixed LTE networks.
- the ongoing cost of operation and maintenance which will include maintaining the required engineering staff, the cost of power, repair of damaged equipment, etc.
- a reasonable allocation of non-network costs, both capital expenditure related to items such as administrative buildings, and non-network operating costs such as HR, finance, legal, customer care, etc.
- the revenues that the USP earns, or can reasonably be expected to earn, from providing the Universal Service and related services.
- intangible benefits associated with being a USP. For example, USP’s brand may become stronger as a result of its being a provider of Universal Services. The TRA would explore whether it would be appropriate, given these intangible benefits associated with being a USP, to offset them against any application from a USP for funding.
6.9 Capital expenditure, especially for network related investment, will occur, to a large extent, towards the beginning of the project. Operating expenditure, on the other hand, will be spread more evently over the duration of the equipment life, and revenues will typically start once the network is operational and customers have been signed up. Revenues are also likely to grow over the term of the project.
6.10 Therefore, the TRA expects to use a standard discounted cash flow (DCF) approach as a measure for the total costs, revenues and hence subsidy requirements.
6.11 In its application for external funding, the USP would have to produce a forward-looking financial model estimating the likely subsidy requirements. The model would have to calculate the costs associated with the capital expenditure with network roll out as well as the non-network costs.
6.12 Revenue modeling should be based on the number of households in an area to be served and take-up assumptions for each group of services to be provided.
7- Affordability and accessibility of the Telecommunication Services in the UAE
7.1 Affordability and accessibility are also important parts of the USO policy.
7.2 All Consumers shall therefore be subject to fair treatment and should not be unduly discriminated against, in comparison to other Consumers of the same service (e.g. based on their geographic location).
7.3 Further, the TRA does not expect average or entry level prices in the market to increase as a result of introducing the USO policy.
7.4 Indeed, the TRA may continue to monitor prices charged by the Licensees, and to benchmark them against those in comparator countries and the costs of service provision. If, on this basis, it identifies a concern that the prices of Telecommunications Services covered by the Universal Service Policy are becoming unaffordable to low income Consumers, the TRA may use its powers under the Law to intervene.
7.5 In relation to accessibility of Telecommunications Services, Licensees should continue “to take into account the special needs and requirements regarding the type of service and Telecommunications Apparatus and ensure non-discrimination when providing such services and apparatus” (as per Article 30 of the Telecommunications Law.)
8- Effective Date
8.1 This Policy shall be effective on the date of issue.
9- Revocation
9.1 The TRA may revise, revoke, or reissue this Policy from time to time as it sees fit.
